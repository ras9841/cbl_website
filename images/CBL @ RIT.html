About the the CBL:

The Computational Biomedicine Laboratory (CBL) is currently directed by Professor Linwei Wang and is part of the Center for Advancing the Study of Cyberinfrastructure (CASCI) at RIT. Funded by the NSF and the NIH, our research focus on computational systems biomedicine that adapts systems approach and concept to biomedical problems of importance to public health. Standing on the intersection between computing and biomedicine, we are seeking and developing methods, algorithms, and systems that will integrate the strength of physics-based modeling with that of data-driven inference/learning methods toward the task of personalized physiological modeling. Integrating physics-based modeling of complex systems into the understanding of data will place the data-driven inference in a domain-realistic context; developing advanced probabilistic inference methods in combination with domain modeling will address the arising issues of uncertainty, robustness, and model-data mismatch. In particular, the driving application area of the CBL lies in the electrophysiology and electromechanics of the heart, aiming for transmural electrophysiological and electromechanical imaging for specific subjects using their physiologic mapping and medical imaging data.

1. Physics-based dynamic model behind Bayesian Statistical Inference

True integration of domain physics and statistical inference requires:

  • A plug-and-play inclusion of domain physical models as a blackbox behind the inference, balancing between efficiency and accuracy
  • Advanced inference methods with theoretical rigor and the ability to handle model uncertainties and model-data mismatch


Research has been published in journals such as IEEE Transactions on Biomedical Engineering and Medical Image Computing and Computer-Assisted Intervention (MICCAI).

2. Low-Dimensional Structure

Low-dimensional structure of a signal can be exploited to overcome the limited information in measurement data, in a way almost similar to the reduction of the degree of freedoms based on the hidden structure of a signal. For applications in biophysical problems with often 3D, unstructured, complex physical domains, adaptation is needed:

  • L0, L1, and total-variation regularizer
  • Deterministic and statistical formulation
  • How does uncertainty matter?


Research has been published in places such as IEEE Transactions on Medical Imaging, IEEE Conference on Computer Vision and Pattern Recognition, and Medical Image Computing and Computer-Assisted Intervention (MICCAI).

3. Physics-based dynamics & Low-Dimensional Structure in Data Inversion

Physics-based dynamics and low-dimensional structure provide complementary knowledge about a system under study:

  • Physics-based models tell us basic knowledge about the domain dynamics of a system, resolving the ambiguity in data inversion.....yet, what if the model is wrong and dominates the inversion due to limited data information?
  • Low-dimensional model hidden in a system helps to address limited data by reducing the “dimension” of unknowns...yet, how to combine it with dynamics


Research has been published in places such as Medical Image Computing and Computer-Assisted Intervention (MICCAI).

4. Quantification and Reduction of Uncertainty

Uncertainty is an integral part of data inversion, stemming from both the assumptions in our prior knowledge and the errors in our measurement data.

  • Statistical framework enabling sensitivity analysis for large complex systems
  • Multiple-model Bayesian inference enabling the analysis of model uncertainties and model-data mismatch on data inversion.


Research has been published in places such as IEEE Transactions on Biomedical Imaging and Medical Image Computing and Computer-Assisted Intervention (MICCAI). This area was also the topic of Azar Rahimi's dissertation: Uncertainty Quantification and Reduction in Cardiac Electrophysiological Imaging.
