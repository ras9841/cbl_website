Machine Learning for Precision Medicine



Project Motivation

An important challenge in disease diagnosis, treatment and prevention is to recognize and address the large variations among patients that may otherwise confound the clinical decision making. While we are at an era with an unprecedented availability of biomedical and healthcare data, it is important for us to be able to tease apart the different factors that contribute to diagnostics versus those that result from inter-subject and intra-subject variations. Developing machine learning algorithms with the ability to recognize and adapt to patient-specific factors have the potential to bring us closer toward the goal of personalized medicine. In the CBL, we are interested in the development of such algorithms as well as their application to important clinical problems.

1. Teasing apart factors

Different approaches are being pursued in the CBL to address the issue of inter-subject variations during machine learning. One approach involves the development of deep network that recognizes representation of diagnostic versus. inter-subject variability features during the learning. Another approach is built upon the concept of multiple task learning to achieve a balance between the learning of a publication model and the learning of a patient-specific model, allowing the transfer of knowledge while learning multiple patient-specific models that share a common structure.


2. Prediction of Ablation Targets for Scar-related Ventricular Tachycardia

Ventricular Tachycardia (VT or V-tach) is a very fast heartbeat caused by a malfunction in one of the heart's ventricles. It is a pulse of more than 100 beats per minute with at least three irregular heartbeats in a row. It can occur with or without heart disease and can cause sudden cardiac arrest. One of the minimally invasive procedures used, for treating arrhythmia, is catheter ablation. This procedure involves finding the "bad" tissue in the heart by trial and error and then destroying that tissue responsible for a faulty electrical pathway in the heart.

Through machine learning techniques described above, we aim to better predict the location of the ablation site for catheter ablation. The initial foray into this started with the use of simple techniques like Support Vector Machines (SVM) and Random Forests, which however did not give a desirably accuracy in predicting the site. This initial interest is now being expanded to more robust techniques such as Multi Task Learning with which we can account for factors such as patient variability along with the individual patient-specific features. Another important aspect of this is the selection of features available from ECG readings which can hugely impact the output. This selection can be done by manually processing and labeling the data, or the use of Deep Learning techniques, where features are extracted automatically from the data itself. This project involves the appropriate processing of the input (ECG) data to a usable format for various algorithms, as well as the mathematical formulation and improvisation of existing machine learning algorithms.

The success of narrowing down the prediction of the ablation site can cause a huge difference in the surgery time for catheter ablation, and promises to be a strong approach for an improvement in computational biomedicine techniques
