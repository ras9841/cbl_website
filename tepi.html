Noninvasive Transmural Electrophysiological Imaging



Project Motivation

Cardiac arrhythmia accounts for increasing risk for all strokes (15%), heart failure, and mortality. The currently used minimally invasive procedure, i.e. catheter ablation, relies on point-by-point contact mapping on the heart surface to construct a 3D map of activation sequence. Such invasive methods make pre-procedural planning infeasible, have limited accuracy and resolution, and involve prolonged and technically challenging mapping process. Transmural electrophysiological imaging (TEPI) is a new concept we introduce to overcome the limitations that have been associated with the current paradigm of invasive mapping. Based on the computational foundation described above, TEPI utilizes noninvasive multi-lead body-surface ECG, followed by an image-based computational algorithm to non-invasively reconstruct the patient-specific intramural electrical dynamics with high resolution.

This noninvasive, transmrual, and high-resolution tool of arrhythmia imaging will provide better pre-procedural planning and targeting for interventiaonl therapies, increasing procedure efficacy and safety while reducing both time and cost. In the long term, this research will contribute to the safe and efficient use of electrophysiological studies in broader routine clinical practice.

Supported by NIH, this project's application interests include ablation of scar-related ventricular arrhythmia, atrial fibrillation, and dyssynchrony.

2. TEPI in Post-infarction Hearts & Scar-related Ventricular Arrhythmia

This research focus on the system development of TEPI to improve its cost-effectiveness in clinical practice. Major developments include:

  • ECG sensing (data acquisition) on the body surface
  • Better and more efficient anatomical modeling (data processing)
  • High-performance computing environment to accelerate TEPI computation.

Research has been published in places such as IEEE Transactions on Medical Imaging, IEEE Conference on Computer Vision and Pattern Recognition, and Medical Image Computing and Computer-Assisted Intervention (MICCAI).

3. Analysis of Atrial Arrhythmias

Atrial Fibrillation (Afib) is the most common type of heart arrhythmia seen in people. We tested the feasibility of using non-invasive computational imaging to compute and visualize such arrhythmias in 3D. A vest of 120 electrodes is used to collect the heart's output signal data. MRI images are used to manually construct a model of the patient's heart. We use Tikhonov regularization to generate the voltages on the heart from the input data. When a person has Afib, his/her left and right atria beat irregularly, often reducing blood flow to the body. Our study concentrates on the identification of electrical rotors and ectopic foci that occur during Afib, using both dominant frequency analysis and phase mapping. Our results will help increase the success of ablation procedures, a method of reversing Afib. In order to validate our pipeline, we conduct our analysis on patients diagnosed with atrial flutter. Our results are then compared to the output of invasive techniques.
